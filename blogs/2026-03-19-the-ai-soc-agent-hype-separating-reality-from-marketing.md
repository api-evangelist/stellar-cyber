									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When a vendor says <strong>“AI-powered SOC,”</strong> they could mean anything from a basic machine learning model trained on historical alert data to a fully autonomous agent that triages, investigates, and responds without human input. Both get marketed identically.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Most of what’s currently sold as an<strong> “AI SOC agent”</strong> falls into one of three categories, and only one of them deserves the label. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first is a chatbot with a security skin. It’s a large language model (LLM) connected to your SIEM that can answer natural language questions about alerts. It doesn’t take actions, doesn’t reason through multi-step investigations, and doesn’t learn from your environment. It’s a query interface, not automation.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The second is a static playbook engine wearing an AI badge. Automated workflows and response playbooks are genuinely valuable, but some vendors have simply rebranded their existing automation as “agentic” because the playbooks now include an LLM step that generates a summary at the end. The orchestration is real. The “agent” label often isn’t.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The third is genuine agentic automation, a system that can analyse signals in context, correlate them across domains, prioritise what matters, and trigger response actions within defined guardrails while keeping humans in the loop for high-risk decisions. </span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This is what marketing should mean. Some platforms have been building this for years on top of unified data, but most vendors jumping on the trend are retrofitting the label onto architectures that were never designed for it.</span></p>								</div>

					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">The Five Questions That Expose Vaporware
</h2>				</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-ad29f02 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Before you buy anything with an “AI agent” on the box, ask these five questions. The answers will tell you whether you’re looking at genuine capability or marketing.</span></p><h3><b>1. Can it do more than summarise?</b></h3><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A chatbot that summarises alerts is useful, but it’s table stakes. The real question is whether the AI can correlate signals across domains, prioritise cases by risk, and surface the full context an analyst needs to act. If the “agent” just restates what your SIEM already told you, it’s not reducing workload.</span></p><h3><b>2. Does it work across your entire stack? </b></h3><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Most vendor-specific “AI agents” only see data from their own products. If your AI can reason about endpoint alerts but is blind to network traffic, identity events, and cloud telemetry, it’s solving a fraction of the problem. Real threats don’t respect vendor boundaries, and neither should your automation.</span></p><h3><b>3. Can it explain its reasoning?</b></h3><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If your AI agent flags an incident as critical but can’t show you the evidence chain that led to that conclusion, your analysts can’t verify it and your auditors can’t review it. A black box that says “trust me” isn’t operational.</span></p><h3><b>4. What happens when it’s wrong? </b></h3><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Every AI system will make mistakes. Does it flag low-confidence decisions for human review? Does it have guardrails that prevent destructive actions without approval? The Gravitee State of AI Agent Security 2026 </span><a href="https://www.gravitee.io/blog/state-of-ai-agent-security-2026-report-when-adoption-outpaces-control" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong>report found</strong> </a> that only 14.4% of organisations report all AI agents going live with full security and IT approval.</p><h3><b>5. What data does it actually see? </b></h3><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If it’s ingesting alerts from a single SIEM but has no visibility into network flows, identity logs, email events, or cloud audit trails, it’s making decisions with a fraction of the picture.</span></p>								</div>
				</div>

					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">What Genuine AI-Driven SOC Automation Looks Like
</h2>				</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-c2fd1aa elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The gap between marketing and reality doesn’t mean AI in the SOC is useless. It means the industry is conflating three different things, and all three have value, they just aren’t the same thing.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">AI-assisted querying helps analysts get answers faster through natural language. This saves time but doesn’t reduce workload because the analyst still has to investigate, decide, and act.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">AI-enhanced detection uses machine learning to improve alert quality at the source. Correlation engines that group related alerts into cases, behavioural models that flag deviations, and prioritisation systems that surface the signals that actually matter. This is where most of the real value lives today, and it’s been quietly improving for years without the “agent” label.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">AI-driven automation is the frontier, where agents reason through investigations, take response actions, and learn from analyst feedback over time. It’s real, but it’s early, and the platforms doing it well are doing it cautiously with human-in-the-loop controls.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Recent </span><a href="https://www.darktrace.com/blog/the-state-of-ai-cybersecurity-2026"><span style="font-weight: 400;">industry research</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> found that only 14% of security professionals allow AI to take independent remediation actions in the SOC with no human in the loop. That number tells you everything about where the industry actually is.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The organisations seeing real results unified their data first, reduced alert noise through better correlation, and layered automation on top of a clean signal. The order matters.</span></p>								</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-9d93526 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Why Data Unification Comes Before AI</h2>				</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-8f87119 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If your data is fragmented across dozens of security tools with dozens of different data models, no amount of AI will fix the underlying problem. You can’t reason about an attack chain that’s scattered across disconnected consoles. Unification, bringing endpoint, network, identity, email, and cloud telemetry into a single data model, is the prerequisite that has to be solved before any meaningful AI automation is possible.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This is why Stellar Cyber built its </span><a href="https://stellarcyber.ai/platform/what-is-open-xdr/">Open XDR</a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> platform the way it did. Rather than replacing your existing security stack, it normalises and enriches data from hundreds of sources, then uses multi-layer AI to correlate individual alerts into investigation-ready cases mapped to the MITRE ATT&amp;CK framework. The correlation happens automatically, which is where the real time savings come from, not from a chatbot summarising alerts one at a time.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">With version 6.3, Stellar Cyber expanded the agentic AI capabilities it’s been building for years with case summaries that automatically explain what happened, why it matters, and what evidence supports the conclusion, alongside automated email phishing triage that catches attacks before they escalate. These aren’t bolted-on features chasing a trend. They’re the result of building AI on top of a unified data foundation from day one.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Customers report 8x improvement in mean time to detect and 20x in mean time to respond. Not because they bolted a chatbot onto a broken workflow, but because they unified the data first and let AI work with a complete picture.</span></p>								</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-0f30ba4 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">The Honest Maturity Model</h2>				</div>
				</div>
				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-8e71f4a elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor">
				<div class="elementor-widget-container">
									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If you’re evaluating AI SOC capabilities, think about it in stages rather than buying into the all-or-nothing framing most vendors push.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stage one is data unification. Get all your telemetry into a single platform with a normalised data model. This alone eliminates the manual correlation work that eats most of your analysts’ time.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stage two is AI-enhanced detection and correlation. Once the data is unified, machine learning can automatically group related alerts into cases, prioritise by risk, and surface the incidents that actually need human attention.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stage three is bounded automation. Specific, well-defined tasks that AI can handle reliably: enriching alerts with threat intelligence, generating investigation summaries, triaging phishing emails. Human-in-the-loop for anything destructive.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stage four is adaptive automation. The system learns from analyst decisions over time, expanding its autonomous capabilities where it’s proven reliable and flagging novel situations for human review. This is where the industry is heading, but pretending we’re already there does a disservice to the teams doing the work.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Most vendors want to sell you stage four, but most security teams haven’t finished stage one.</span></p>								</div>
